◆ Start here

The five mindsets of a good assistant

Before any instrument or technique, this is the frame of mind that separates a helpful assistant from an invaluable one. Everything else in this guide is a way of living out these five habits.

1

Think proactively

Anticipate the surgeon's next step and have the instrument, sponge, or suction ready before it's asked for. Watch the case, not just your hands.

2

Protect the sterile field

Infection control is vigilance made routine. Be meticulous and consistent every single time — the field doesn't get "mostly" sterile.

3

Communicate clearly

Understand instructions fast, confirm when unsure, and speak up when you see something — a slipping retractor, a count that feels off.

4

Stay calm under pressure

Fast-paced or unexpected moments are when steadiness matters most. Your composure keeps the whole room's tempo even.

5

Keep learning

Techniques, instruments, and preferences change by surgeon and by case. Reflect after each one; treat every case as a rep.

The core idea

Skate to where the puck is going to be. A great assistant isn't reacting to the current moment — they're already positioned for the next one. Hold that image through everything below.

◆ The north star

The tenets of Halsted

When the surgeon is operating, they're constantly thinking about the principles William Stewart Halsted laid down for gentle, careful surgical technique — the tenets that still define good surgery. A great assistant knows them too, and to the best of their ability, serves them. Almost everything else in this guide is really one of these tenets in action.

1 Gentle handling of tissue

Tissue that's crushed, torn, or roughly handled heals worse. Every touch should be as delicate as the task allows.

You: retract with the least force that gives the view; grip tissue gently (a sponge adds grip without a hard pinch); never yank or let a retractor dig in.

2 Meticulous hemostasis

Control every bleeder. A dry field is a safe field — and a visible one.

You: keep the field dry so bleeders show themselves — suction, targeted blotting, and pressure — and have the cautery, clamp, or suture ready before it's asked for.

3 Preservation of blood supply

Tissue heals only if its blood supply survives. Protect it.

You: avoid over-retracting or kinking pedicles; keep retraction off vascular structures; don't lean on or clamp tissue that doesn't need it.

4 Strict aseptic technique

Keep the field sterile to prevent infection — no exceptions.

You: this is your sterile-technique discipline in action — only sterile touches sterile, every single time.

5 Minimum tension on tissues

Tissue held or closed under tension tears and breaks down. Keep it relaxed.

You: give steady, even exposure so the surgeon isn't fighting the tissue; hold edges together gently during suturing; follow the suture without over-tightening the tails.

6 Accurate tissue apposition

Layers that are lined up precisely heal cleanly. Approximate, don't strangulate.

You: present and hold edges so they meet cleanly; keep the field tidy and visible so the layers are clear; manage suture so each throw lands where intended.

7 Obliteration of dead space

Close potential spaces so fluid or hematoma can't collect in them.

You: help expose the deep layers for closure and keep those spaces dry, so nothing is left open or missed.

Why it matters for you

You don't hold the needle driver, but you shape whether these tenets are met on every case. Gentle retraction, a dry and tidy field, protected tissue, and steady exposure are how the assistant lives out Halsted's principles. Serve the tenets, and you're already assisting well.

◆ Never idle

What to do while you're "just standing there"

Here's a secret from the surgeon's side of the table: there is almost never nothing to do. Idle time is the assistant's biggest opportunity. Every quiet moment is a chance to make the case safer, cleaner, and faster — to move the case forward instead of waiting to be told.

Before the case

Set the room up to win
  • Lights — position and focus the overhead lights (and any headlight) on the field before the surgeon reaches for them.
  • Screens & monitors — angle displays so the surgeon and team can see; confirm imaging and cameras are up.
  • SCDs — sequential compression devices on and actually running for DVT prophylaxis.
  • Positioning & stirrups — help position the patient, set the stirrups, pad pressure points, secure safety straps.
  • Prep & shaving — clip or shave as needed and assist with the skin prep.
  • Supplies & instruments — confirm the needed instruments are present, complete, and working, and that special supplies are open and ready.

During the case

Keep it moving, keep it tidy
  • Hold pressure & retraction — anticipate it and provide it without being asked.
  • Manage suture tails — follow the suture, keep tails clear and untangled, cut to length, and have the next one ready or loaded.
  • Keep a tidy field — organize the Mayo, wipe instruments clean, clear used sponges, keep the working surface uncluttered.
  • Anticipate the next step — have the next instrument, sponge, or suction in hand before the request comes.
  • Stay count-aware — keep sponges trackable and flag anything added to the field.
  • Watch & communicate — notice when a supply is running low or the view is degrading, and act on it.
The rule of thumb

If you find yourself waiting, you're probably missing something. There is always a way to make the case more efficient or move it forward — anticipating the next step, tidying the field, managing a suture, or getting the next thing ready. An assistant who's never idle is one the surgeon never has to manage.

◆ Foundation

Sterile technique foundations

You can't help the surgery if you contaminate it. Sterile technique is the non-negotiable baseline — learn it cold before you touch a retractor. The governing principle is simple to say and takes practice to feel: only sterile touches sterile. Anything that touches a non-sterile surface is now non-sterile, and there is no in-between.

✓ Do

  • Keep your gloved hands in front of you, above waist level, and within the sterile field at all times.
  • Treat the top of a draped table as sterile only on its surface — anything below table edge is non-sterile.
  • Keep the sterile field in your sight; if you can't see it, you can't vouch for it.
  • Assume the outer 1-inch border of any drape or wrapper is non-sterile.
  • Move slowly and deliberately; face the field when passing others.

✕ Don't

  • Never turn your back on a sterile field or reach across it.
  • Don't drop your hands below your waist or tuck them under your arms.
  • Don't lean over the field or let unsterile items (cords, hair, ID badges) dip into it.
  • Don't assume — if you're unsure whether something is sterile, it isn't. Say so and have it replaced.
  • Don't reach for a dropped item; consider it contaminated.

For a brand-new assistant, this is the first thing to over-learn. Confidence at the field comes from never having to wonder whether you just broke sterility — because your habits made it automatic.

◆ The heart of the job

Assisting the surgery: exposure & retraction

This is what "assisting" really means. Think of yourself as the surgeon's second set of hands and eyes. Your job is to create and hold the exact view the surgeon needs to see and dissect in — and to keep re-creating it as the operation moves.

Positioning is everything

You are constantly adjusting retractors or tissue to give the best possible view, while being careful not to cause unnecessary trauma. Good exposure isn't about pulling hard — it's about pulling in the right direction so the plane the surgeon is working in opens up cleanly.

Balance tension and visibility

This is the skill that takes the longest to develop, and it's worth naming precisely:

The tension trade-off

Too much tension tears or bruises tissue and can cause bleeding that ruins the view. Too little lets tissue fall back and obscures the target. A good assistant develops a feel for how much force is appropriate — and adjusts the instant the surgeon's line of sight changes.

Practically: watch the surgeon's instruments and eyes, not the clock. When they shift deeper or to a new angle, your retraction should shift with them, not a beat later.

Anticipate the steps

If you know the surgeon is heading deeper, you're already thinking about which instruments come next, how you'll re-set your retractors, and where your hands need to be. This is the "puck" mindset applied moment to moment — the difference between an assistant the surgeon has to direct and one who simply appears where they're needed.

Mind the ergonomics — for both of you

Good exposure means less fatigue and fewer mistakes across a long case. Keep your own posture sustainable (you can't hold a shaking retractor for two hours) and position so the surgeon isn't fighting your hands for room. Where it's an option, a self-retaining retractor can hold steady broad exposure and free your hands for finer help — but handheld retraction lets you make the constant micro-adjustments a self-retaining system can't.

Global → local → fine

Great assistants move fluidly between three "zooms": the global view of the whole field, the local view of the region being worked, and the fine view of the exact plane being dissected. Much of the art is knowing which zoom the surgeon needs right now — and setting exposure for that one.

◆ Know your tools

Knowing the retractors

You don't need to memorize a catalog, but you should recognize the workhorses of pelvic and vaginal surgery on sight and know what each one is for. Most are handheld — you hold and adjust them for maximum control — while self-retaining systems hold themselves for steady broad exposure. Your hands (with a sponge for grip) count too.

Handheld · right-angle

Jackson retractor

A right-angle handheld vaginal retractor with a broad blade, used to hold a vaginal wall back — often the anterior wall — and open the field. A dependable go-to for steady, controllable exposure in vaginal cases.

Handheld · narrow blade

Breisky-Navratil

A long, slightly concave handheld vaginal retractor with a ridged handle and comfortable grip. Excellent for deep, narrow retraction — reaching in to hold a sidewall back without crowding the field.

Handheld · versatile

Deaver

A flat, thin, curved-end blade in various widths and depths. Smooth edges limit tissue trauma. A general-purpose retractor for pulling a wall or organ aside to open the working space.

Handheld · deep pelvis

Heaney / Heaney-Simon

Right-angle style vaginal retractors built for deep pelvic exposure; lighted variants (Heaney-Simon, Miyazaki) add illumination where the field is dark and far from the surgeon's headlight.

When you're new, learn each surgeon's preferred names and setups — the same instrument may be called different things, and every surgeon has favorites for a given step. Ask early, then remember.

◆ The judgment call

Suction vs. lap sponge vs. Ray-Tec

These three are not just "things that soak up blood." Each solves a different exposure problem. The real skill is matching the tool to what the surgeon needs to see next — you're often helping define the plane of dissection as much as controlling blood. This matters most in a narrow field like the vaginal/deep-pelvic space.

Tool Reach for it when… Role in exposure & retraction Watch out for
Suction immediate clarity Fluid is accumulating faster than a sponge can manage, or you need the field kept continuously clear. Best for re-establishing the view quickly after an instrument move. Deep pelvis: clears blood pooling in dependent spaces to reveal fine planes. A light suction tip evacuates fluid without crowding the working space — and the tip itself can double as a retractor, especially for the vaginal sidewalls or labia. Used carelessly it can "steal" tissue into the tip or obscure the target. Keep it light and off delicate structures.
Lap sponge pressure & packing You need broad blunt pressure, packing, or retraction, or a "big wipe" to make wound edges visible or clear the whole field — not precision. In vaginal cases, longer packs help keep bowel out of the field. Also a blunt dissector and a fast way to apply direct pressure on diffuse bleeding. A broad-purpose tool: absorbs, applies pressure, and acts as a blunt retractor holding tissue out of the way over a wide area. Bulky in tight spaces. Many surgeons avoid lots of small loose sponges here because they're hard to track — see counts below.
Ray-Tec (small sponge) precision You need small wipes, focused blotting, or to hold pressure between steps — e.g. between suture throws — where a lap would be clumsy. The "precision" option when the goal is to reveal a plane, not just remove fluid. Ideal in a tight vaginal field where space is at a premium. Clears a small area without taking up room, preserving the line of sight. Also gives extra grip on tissue when you're using your own hands as retractors. Small and easy to lose in the field — must be radiopaque and counted. Never cut or repurpose one.
The one question to ask yourself

"What will most efficiently create the surgeon's next view?" Sometimes that's suction for instant clarity, sometimes a lap for pressure or blunt retraction, sometimes a small sponge for controlled, localized exposure. In a narrow vaginal field, exposure usually comes from combining retractors, packing, suction, and targeted sponge work — shifting from broad exposure to a narrow, high-tension view of the exact plane being dissected.

◆ Patient safety

Sponge counts & keeping nothing behind

Every sponge in the field is something that must come back out. A retained surgical item is a serious, preventable harm — which is why counting is a rigid, shared ritual, not a formality. As an assistant you support it by keeping sponges trackable and never introducing an uncounted one.

When counts happen

Counts are done at set moments — a baseline before incision, again as each cavity/layer is closed, and at skin closure — plus any time items are added or staff change. The scrub and circulator count together, out loud, both seeing each item.

How sponges are handled

Only radiopaque (X-ray-detectable) sponges go into the field. Each is separated and confirmed. Counted sponges are never cut, and never used for skin prep, dressings, or packing left in the patient.

Your part as the assistant

Keep sponges organized in the field so they can be found and counted. If you place packing, tell the team and remember it's there. Prefer trackable sponges over lots of small loose ones. And if a count is off — speak up immediately and don't close. A wrong count is everyone's problem to solve together.

◆ From the ground up

Your learning path, step by step

For someone with no OR exposure, build both knowledge and intuition in stages. Each step earns the next — don't rush past the foundations.

Before the OR

Orientation outside the room

Start with the basics on your own time and terms: surgical instruments by name and use, essential anatomy, and sterile technique. Handle the instruments, learn the retractors above, get comfortable before the pressure is real.

Watch first

Observe with a purpose

Watch cases with a specific focus: how experienced assistants manage exposure and anticipate needs. Don't just watch the surgeon — watch the assistant's hands and when they move.

Hands on, simple

Start with simple tasks

Gradually take on holding retractors and handing instruments, with coaching on where to put your hands and how much tension to apply. Build the physical feel before the judgment calls.

Coached reps

Real-time feedback

Get small corrections and genuine praise during cases. Little adjustments — "rotate that a touch," "ease off" — build the tension intuition faster than anything, and confidence right alongside it.

After every case

Reflect and refine

After each surgery, review what went well and what to improve. This is where reps turn into intuition — and where she starts to move naturally between the global, local, and fine views.

◆ Keep this handy

Quick-reference cheat sheet

The one-glance version to review before a case or when you need a fast reminder at the field.

Mindset in one line each

  • Anticipate — have it ready before it's asked.
  • Stay sterile — only sterile touches sterile.
  • Communicate — confirm, and speak up.
  • Stay calm — your composure sets the tempo.
  • Keep learning — every case is a rep.

Halsted, served by the assistant

  • Gentle tissue handling — least force that works.
  • Dry field for hemostasis — suction, blot, pressure.
  • Protect blood supply — no kinked pedicles.
  • Sterile always; low tension; clean apposition; no dead space.

Exposure checklist

  • Pull in the right direction, not just hard.
  • Enough tension to see, not enough to tear.
  • Re-set the instant the surgeon's angle changes.
  • Protect tissue; protect your own posture.
  • Set exposure for the zoom they need now.

Never idle

  • Before: lights, screens, SCDs, positioning/stirrups, prep, check instruments.
  • During: hold pressure, manage suture tails, tidy the field, ready the next step.
  • Waiting? You're probably missing something.

Which tool?

  • Suction → clears fluid fast; tip doubles as a sidewall/labia retractor.
  • Lap sponge → broad pressure, packing, and "big wipes" to clear the field.
  • Ray-Tec → small wipes, pressure between stitch throws, grip for hand retraction.
  • Always ask: what creates the next view?

Safety non-negotiables

  • Only radiopaque sponges in the field.
  • Announce any packing you place.
  • Never cut or repurpose a counted sponge.
  • Count off? Speak up. Don't close.
A note on use: This is an orientation and mentoring aid to build understanding and vocabulary for a new assistant. It doesn't replace hands-on training, your facility's protocols, or the surgeon's direct instruction — always defer to those and to current institutional policy.
